\subsection{Geometry Generation}
\label{sec:geometry}

Ahmed body geometries are generated programmatically using a headless FreeCAD script (\texttt{generate\_ahmed\_freecad.py}) that reads design parameters from environment variables and writes a watertight STL surface. The body dimensions follow the Lienhart \& Becker~\cite{lienhart2002} reference geometry: $L=1044$~mm, $W=389$~mm, $H=288$~mm on four cylindrical legs of radius $R_\mathrm{leg}=15$~mm. The slant begins at $x=822$~mm from the nose. The nose fillet radius $R_\mathrm{nose}$ is applied to both the top and bottom leading edges, clamped to $H/2-5$~mm to prevent arc intersection. Ground clearance is fixed at the nominal Lienhart value $h_0=50.8$~mm for all cases.

Figure~\ref{fig:ahmed_schematic} shows the parameterised side profile. The rear slant spans the final 222~mm of the body ($x=822$--$1044$~mm); the underbody diffuser ramp begins at $x=869$~mm (just aft of the rear legs) and likewise terminates at the rear face, so the two ramps overlap in $x$ and together set the rear face height. The slant angle $\alpha_\mathrm{slant}$ and diffuser angle $\alpha_\mathrm{diff}$ are the active design variables; $R_\mathrm{nose}$ and $h$ are fixed.

For half-domain (symmetry) runs, the body STL is used directly without mirroring — the Ahmed body is symmetric about the centreplane $y=0$ by construction, so the full STL already provides the half-body once the domain is restricted to $y \geq 0$.

\subsection{Mesh Generation}
\label{sec:mesh}

Meshing uses cfMesh~\cite{cfmesh} \texttt{cartesianMesh} to generate an unstructured Cartesian hex-dominant background grid, followed by \texttt{generateBoundaryLayers} for prismatic near-wall cells. This replaces the earlier blockMesh/snappyHexMesh approach, which was abandoned due to difficulty controlling cell quality at the body-leg junction.

\subsubsection{Computational Domain}

The full domain extends $5L$ upstream ($x=-5.22$~m), $15L$ downstream ($x=15.66$~m), laterally $\pm 3.75W$ ($y=\pm 1.95$~m), and $10H$ above ($z=2.88$~m). For symmetry cases the domain is restricted to $y \in [0, 1.95]$~m with the $y=0$ plane treated as a symmetry boundary.

\subsubsection{Refinement Strategy}

Three nested refinement boxes are applied around the body (Table~\ref{tab:refinement_boxes}). Surface-local refinement of 10~mm is applied to the \texttt{ahmed\_body} patch via \texttt{localRefinement}. All cell sizes are halved in the symmetry cases to maintain the same near-body resolution.

\subsubsection{Boundary Layers}

Prismatic layers are grown on \texttt{ahmed\_body} only: 3 layers, thickness ratio 1.2, maximum first-layer thickness 50~mm. The all-$y^+$ wall treatment is used (see \S\ref{sec:wall_treatment}), so no explicit $y^+$ target is imposed; cfMesh grows layers until the quality optimiser stalls. The \texttt{allowDiscontinuity 1} flag permits layer-count jumps at patch boundaries to avoid degenerate cells at the leg--body junction.

\subsection{Reference Area Correction}
\label{sec:aref}

The frontal reference area is $A_\mathrm{ref} = W \times H = 0.389 \times 0.288 = 0.1121$~m$^2$ for the full body. Force coefficient definitions in OpenFOAM's \texttt{forceCoeffs} function use:
\[
    C_d = \frac{F_d}{\tfrac{1}{2}\rho U_\infty^2 A_\mathrm{ref}}
\]
For half-domain (symmetry) simulations only half the body is present, so the drag force is also halved. Setting $A_\mathrm{ref} = 0.056$~m$^2$ (half the full area) compensates exactly and yields the correct full-body $C_d$. All cases in this study use $A_\mathrm{ref} = 0.056$~m$^2$.

\textit{Note:} an earlier version of the mesh convergence study used $A_\mathrm{ref} = 0.112$~m$^2$ in the full-domain cases, which halved all reported $C_d$ and $C_l$ values. The corrected values are those in Table~\ref{tab:mesh_levels}.

\subsection{Wall Treatment}
\label{sec:wall_treatment}

An all-$y^+$ (blended) wall treatment is used throughout, which remains valid across a wide range of $y^+$ values without requiring a fixed target:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \texttt{nutUSpaldingWallFunction} — blended $\nu_t$ using Spalding's law of the wall
    \item \texttt{kLowReWallFunction} — $k$ wall BC that transitions smoothly from low-$y^+$ to high-$y^+$ behaviour
    \item \texttt{omegaWallFunction} — standard $\omega$ wall BC
\end{itemize}

This avoids the requirement for $y^+ \approx 1$ (which would demand much finer near-wall cells and larger meshes) while still resolving boundary layers reasonably at the moderate Reynolds number of this study ($Re = 2.78 \times 10^6$).

Figure~\ref{fig:wall_yplus} shows the $y^+$ distribution on the \texttt{ahmed\_body} patch at the L2 optimum design point. Measured $y^+$ values are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:yplus_summary}.

Both meshes operate in the buffer-to-log-law regime where the blended Spalding formulation is valid. Elevated $y^+$ at the leg bases (${\sim}150$--$220$) reflects stagnation-point impingement; the slant face operates at $y^+ < 15$ on both meshes, which is within the blended formulation's valid range. However, it is important to note that slant \emph{separation onset} — the primary mechanism generating the downforce signal and thus most of the variation in the objective $f_1$ — is predicted at wall-function resolution rather than with a resolved viscous sublayer. This is the least reliable element of the model: kOmegaSST with Spalding wall functions is known to delay separation onset relative to low-$y^+$ formulations, and the L2 vs L3 discrepancy in $C_l$ (\S\ref{sec:l3_validation}) is partly attributable to this effect.

\subsection{Turbulence Model}
\label{sec:turbulence_model}

The $k$-$\omega$~SST model~\cite{menter1994} is used for all DoE evaluations. A comparison run with realisable $k$-$\varepsilon$ on the L2 mesh gave $C_d = 0.324$ versus $C_d = 0.330$ for SST (both relative to the Lienhart value of 0.299). SST is preferred because:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item It is better validated for bluff bodies with mild adverse pressure gradients
    \item Its $\omega$-based near-wall formulation is more consistent with the all-$y^+$ wall functions used here
    \item Benchmark studies~\cite{lienhart2002} routinely use SST as the reference RANS model for the Ahmed body
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Iteration Count and Force Averaging}

A convergence study on the L2 symmetry mesh showed that the pressure residual stalls at $\sim 2 \times 10^{-4}$ from iteration 300 onwards — tighter residual targets are not achievable due to inherent low-frequency unsteadiness in the separated wake under RANS. The force coefficients oscillate with amplitude $\pm 0.005$ around a stable mean throughout. Running beyond 800 iterations does not reduce this oscillation or shift the mean.

Therefore each DoE evaluation uses \textbf{800 iterations}, and $C_d$ and $C_l$ are extracted as the \textbf{mean of the last 200 iterations} (iters 600--800) rather than the final single value. This reduces per-case wall time from 4.3~min (1500 iters) to $\approx$2.2~min at 8 cores, giving a 30-case DoE in approximately 66~minutes total.

\subsection{Half-Plane Symmetry}
\label{sec:symmetry}

To reduce DoE solve time, a half-domain mesh exploiting the geometric symmetry of the Ahmed body about $y=0$ was investigated. Two meshing strategies were attempted:

\paragraph{Strategy 1 — cfMesh half-domain (abandoned).}
A half-domain STL was generated by clipping the body at $y=0$ and capping the open cross-section. cfMesh was then run with the half-domain box. This produced 590K cells but with 59--74 severely non-orthogonal faces ($>70°$) concentrated at the body--symmetry plane intersection, causing simpleFoam to crash on the first iteration regardless of initialisation strategy. The root cause is cfMesh creating doubly-trimmed cells where the body surface terminates exactly at the Cartesian boundary plane.

\paragraph{Strategy 2 — topoSet/subsetMesh cut (adopted).}
The validated full-domain L2 mesh is cut at $y=0$ using OpenFOAM's \texttt{topoSet} (selecting all cells with centroid $y \geq 0$) followed by \texttt{subsetMesh}. The exposed faces at $y=0$ are assigned the \texttt{symmetry} patch type. This approach:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item Inherits the L2 mesh quality (1 severely non-orthogonal face, max skewness 3.07)
    \item Yields 435\,920 cells — exactly 48.6\% of the L2 full mesh
    \item Requires no re-meshing when the domain geometry changes
\end{itemize}

The symmetry mesh is used for all 30 DoE evaluations. Validation against the full L2 result ($C_d = 0.330$) is in \S\ref{sec:symmetry_validation}.

\subsection{RANS Design-of-Experiments}
\label{sec:rans_doe}

The DoE proceeds in two phases.

\paragraph{Phase 1 — 4-variable screening (30 points).}
A 30-point Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) over four design variables (Table~\ref{tab:design_variables_phase1}) provides initial training data and screens for low-sensitivity parameters.

\paragraph{Phase 2 — 2-variable dense sweep (50 points).}
Following Sobol sensitivity analysis (see \S\ref{sec:sobol}), $R_\mathrm{nose}$ and $h$ are fixed at their nominal values ($R_\mathrm{nose}=100$~mm, $h=50.8$~mm). A fresh 50-point LHS over $(\alpha_\mathrm{slant},\, \alpha_\mathrm{diff})$ alone provides the dense training set used to fit the final surrogate. The 50-point 2-D design gives 25 points per dimension, well above the 10× rule of thumb.

The LHS is generated using \texttt{scipy.stats.qmc.LatinHypercube}, selecting the best of 10 candidate designs by centred $L_2$-discrepancy. Each case uses the L2 symmetry mesh with the solver settings in \S\ref{sec:solver}. The objective function is:
\[
    f = C_d + \tfrac{1}{3} C_l
\]
following standard aerodynamic drag-lift trade-off practice for ground vehicles.

\subsection{Sobol Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:sobol}

After fitting a GP surrogate to the Phase~1 data, Sobol variance-based sensitivity indices~\cite{saltelli2010} are computed to rank the influence of each design variable on $f$. The GP surrogate replaces the CFD solver in the sensitivity analysis, requiring no additional simulations. A Saltelli sample of $N = 2048$ points ($N(2d+2) = 20\,480$ total evaluations of the GP mean) is used with the \texttt{SALib} library~\cite{salib}. Both first-order ($S_1$) and total-order ($S_T$) indices are reported. $S_T < 0.05$ is used as the threshold for variable elimination.

\subsection{GP Surrogate and Bayesian Optimisation}
\label{sec:surrogate}

\paragraph{Phase 1 — Single-fidelity L2 surrogate.}
A Gaussian Process (GP) is trained on the 50-point Phase~2 DoE using a Mat\'{e}rn-$5/2$ ARD kernel and a \texttt{WhiteKernel} noise term. Separate GPs are fitted for $C_d$ and $C_l$; inputs are standardised to zero mean and unit variance (\texttt{StandardScaler}). Leave-one-out cross-validation validates accuracy before deployment. The acquisition function is Expected Improvement (EI), maximised via differential evolution over the 2-D design space to direct additional L2 evaluations. A total of 20 BO iterations are run.

\paragraph{Phase 2 — Multi-fidelity co-Kriging correction.}
The single-fidelity L2 GP is used as the low-fidelity (LF) component of the additive Kennedy \& O'Hagan~\cite{kennedy2000} co-Kriging model introduced in \S\ref{sec:introduction}:
\[
    f_\mathrm{HF}(\mathbf{x}) = f_\mathrm{LF}(\mathbf{x}) + \delta(\mathbf{x})
\]
The correction $\delta(\mathbf{x}) = y_\mathrm{L3} - \hat{y}_\mathrm{L2}$ is computed at each of the 8 L3 anchor locations (see \S\ref{sec:l3_anchors}), and an independent GP with Mat\'{e}rn-$5/2$ kernel is fitted to these residuals. This produces a corrected high-fidelity (HF) surrogate whose predictions account for the systematic L2--L3 fidelity gap. The combined predictive uncertainty is:
\[
    \sigma^2_\mathrm{HF}(\mathbf{x}) \approx \sigma^2_\mathrm{LF}(\mathbf{x}) + \sigma^2_\delta(\mathbf{x})
\]
The MF objective $f_1^\mathrm{HF} = C_{d,\mathrm{HF}} + \tfrac{1}{3}C_{l,\mathrm{HF}}$ is minimised via differential evolution on the corrected surrogate surface, and the resulting MF optimum is verified with a single L3 simulation.

\subsection{L3 Anchor Design and Fidelity Assignment}
\label{sec:l3_anchors}

The fidelity assignment for co-Kriging is:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Low-fidelity (LF):} L2 mesh (${\sim}436$K half-domain cells, ${\sim}10$~min per solve) — used for all 50 Phase~2 DoE points and 20 BO iterations.
    \item \textbf{High-fidelity (HF):} L3 mesh (${\sim}912$K half-domain cells, ${\sim}25$~min per solve) — used exclusively for the 8 co-Kriging anchor points and the MF optimum verification.
\end{itemize}

Eight anchor points are placed in the active 2-D design space to train the correction GP (Table~\ref{tab:l3_anchors}). The anchor set is constructed to: (i) include the L2 BO optimum location, where the fidelity gap is most consequential; (ii) span both the sub-critical ($\alpha_\mathrm{slant} < 30^\circ$) and super-critical ($\alpha_\mathrm{slant} > 30^\circ$) regimes, since the correction is expected to vary strongly across the critical angle; and (iii) provide reasonable spatial coverage in the diffuser direction.
